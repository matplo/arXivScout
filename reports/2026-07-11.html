Heavy-ion collision papers

Theory

Azimuthal momentum isotropization in the Quark-Gluon Plasma thermalization

strong

Sergio Barrera Cabodevila, Xiaojian Du, Carlos A. Salgado, Bin Wu. arXiv:2607.07796

Summary: The authors study how initial azimuthal momentum anisotropies relax using the Boltzmann Equation in Diffusion Approximation. They report a hierarchy in relaxation times across harmonic coefficients and a time evolution of pT-dependent harmonics that can qualitatively resemble experimental trends in a phenomenologically motivated setup.

Why it matters: Small-system collectivity keeps pressure on the assumption that initial momentum anisotropies are quickly erased. A controlled kinetic-theory treatment helps test how much memory of the initial state can survive before hydrodynamic response dominates.

Critical assessment: Strong and directly relevant. The phenomenological comparison is suggestive, but the next test is embedding this mechanism in a full event-by-event modeling chain with realistic initial conditions and final-state analysis.

A symmetry-based resolution of pseudo-gauge ambiguities in local equilibrium

strong

Carlos Hoyos. arXiv:2607.08569

Summary: The paper treats pseudo-gauge and improvement-term ambiguities in local-equilibrium density operators as spurious symmetry freedoms associated with currents carrying zero total charge. It then proposes defining local equilibrium through currents tied to genuine symmetries, making the density operator invariant under improvement transformations.

Why it matters: Spin polarization in heavy-ion collisions depends sensitively on how angular momentum, spin, and local equilibrium are defined. This paper targets a real conceptual source of convention dependence in polarization phenomenology.

Critical assessment: Strong as a formal theory contribution. Its practical impact will depend on whether the prescription becomes usable in hydrodynamic or kinetic calculations that can be compared with polarization data.

Phenomenology

Analysis of Nuclear Fragmentation Functions for Pions with A and nu Dependence

strong

Mengyang Li, Zijian Ye, Jun Gao, Xiaomin Shen, Hongxi Xing, Yuxiang Zhao. arXiv:2607.08669

Summary: This work extracts pion nuclear fragmentation functions while fitting vacuum fragmentation and nuclear modifications together. Nuclear effects are parameterized in mass number A, parton energy nu in the target rest frame, and hadron energy fraction z, with SIDIS nuclear data and NLO pp/pA predictions compared to ALICE.

Why it matters: Nuclear hadronization baselines matter for EIC, pA, and heavy-ion interpretation. Explicit A and energy dependence is a useful step toward separating cold-nuclear hadronization effects from hot-medium modification.

Critical assessment: Strong phenomenology with practical value. The constrained z range is an important limitation, so extrapolations outside the fitted region should be treated cautiously.

Heavy quark coalescence probability in the presence of a potential

mixed-positive

Taesoo Song, Jiaxing Zhao. arXiv:2607.08707

Summary: The paper studies how a phenomenological heavy-quark potential affects coalescence probability. The potential is tuned to reproduce vacuum pseudoscalar and vector heavy-meson masses, and the authors find enhanced coalescence with probabilities remaining near unity under moderate in-medium potential modification.

Why it matters: Coalescence is central to charm and bottom hadronization in heavy-ion collisions, especially when interpreting heavy-flavor yields and flow. A more explicit potential dependence is a useful ingredient.

Critical assessment: Useful but narrow. The paper clarifies one hadronization ingredient; it does not by itself validate a full heavy-flavor transport description in an evolving QGP.

AI/ML use

Transformer-based machine learning using low-level calorimeter signals for collimated photon identification at collider experiments

strong

Gabriel Matos, Lauren Larson, Abhilasha Dave, Maria Bressan, Azal Amer, Cindy Liu, Nikiforos Nikiforou, Jonathan Long, Timothy Andeen, John Parsons, Julia Gonski. arXiv:2607.08175

Summary: The paper compares high-level shower-shape methods with cell-level ML architectures for separating highly collimated diphoton signatures from isolated photons. A Transformer gives the best classification performance among six architectures, while an MLP-Mixer is presented as a lower-resource trigger-level candidate; the Transformer also performs invariant-mass regression from calorimeter cells.

Why it matters: Heavy-ion and HEP analyses both struggle with photon/pi0/eta backgrounds and dense electromagnetic environments. The cell-level approach is credible transfer material for detector-level PID, photon tagging, and future trigger studies.

Critical assessment: Strong as a collider ML study because it uses low-level detector information and compares multiple architectures. Transfer to heavy-ion conditions would still require occupancy, pileup/underlying-event, and calibration stress tests.

AI/ML use cases in physics

Optimal-Transport-Based Cell Resampling for Negative and Pathological Event Weights

strong

Regan Doherty, Lauren Hay, Rishabh Jain, Matt LeBlanc, Julia Marrinan, Camille Mauceri, Jennifer Roloff. arXiv:2607.08723

Method and result: The authors use optimal-transport metrics, including Energy Mover's Distance and a spectral variant, for local resampling of negative and pathologically large Monte Carlo weights. In NLO QCD samples, the approach reportedly reduces bias relative to earlier cell-resampling methods and introduces Cross-Section Mover's Distance as an unbinned bias diagnostic.

Likely use case: Directly relevant to HEP event generation and analysis production, including jet and heavy-ion baseline workflows where weighted samples can dominate storage and CPU costs.

Critical assessment: Strong and practical. It is computational statistics rather than neural ML, but it addresses a real production bottleneck; the preliminary-version status argues for independent reproduction before deployment.

Transformer-based machine learning using low-level calorimeter signals for collimated photon identification at collider experiments

strong

Gabriel Matos et al. (11 authors). arXiv:2607.08175

Method and result: Cell-level calorimeter inputs improve identification of collimated diphoton signatures over high-level shower-shape features, with a Transformer strongest and an MLP-Mixer positioned as a resource-conscious option.

Likely use case: Photon/pi0 separation, trigger studies, and dense-environment calorimeter reconstruction in collider experiments, including potential heavy-ion adaptations after occupancy validation.

Critical assessment: Strong transfer candidate, but not yet a heavy-ion result. The decisive test would be performance under central heavy-ion underlying event and realistic detector calibrations.

DeepPySR - A Symbolic Regression Framework with Dynamic Pruning, Pareto Selection, and Hierarchical Composition for Real-World Scientific Discovery

mixed-positive

Fuling Chen, Kevin Vinsen, Phillip Melton, Rae-Chi Huang. arXiv:2607.08150

Method and result: DeepPySR combines dynamic variable pruning, an exponential Pareto selection rule, and hierarchical symbolic composition. It reports improvements over PySR on several applied datasets and includes four Feynman physics benchmarks.

Likely use case: Interpretable surrogate discovery in nuclear structure, detector calibration, reaction modeling, or phenomenological formula search where closed-form expressions are more useful than black-box predictors.

Critical assessment: Interesting but generic. Feynman benchmarks are a sanity check, not evidence that the method handles noisy collider or nuclear data with systematics.

Weak or overclaimed papers worth caution

Secondary Hadron-Nucleus Collisions of Short-Lived Hadrons in Ultra-Relativistic Fixed-Target Heavy-Ion Interactions

caution

Sanatan Digal, P. S. Saumia, Ajit M. Srivastava. arXiv:2607.08658

Why caution: The space-time mechanism is clever: boosted forward hadrons from one nuclear encounter in a solid target may survive to interact with another nucleus. But the abstract-level case is still feasibility-oriented and needs production spectra, survival probabilities, target geometry, rates, detector acceptance, and background modeling before it can be treated as a realistic experimental opportunity.
