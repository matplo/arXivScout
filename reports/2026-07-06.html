Heavy-ion collision papers

Theory

Probing hot QCD medium with heavy quarkonium in small and large collision systems

strong

Jiamin Liu, Baoyi Chen; 2 authors. arXiv:2607.02042

Summary: The paper evolves charmonium and bottomonium states in complex in-medium heavy-quark potentials using a time-dependent Schrodinger-equation framework. It describes excited-to-ground-state suppression ratios in p-Pb at 8.16 TeV and applies the same framework to bottomonium ratios in Pb-Pb at 5.02 TeV.

Why it matters: Quarkonium ratios suppress common production and cold-nuclear-matter uncertainties, making them useful final-state probes. A unified description across flavors and systems strengthens the small-system hot-medium case.

Critical assessment: Strong and directly relevant. The observable choice is clean, but ratio agreement should still be read as evidence for, not proof of, a transient QGP in p-Pb because competing small-system explanations remain difficult to eliminate fully.

Gluon radiation from a QCD antenna with realistic parton-medium interactions

strong

Carlota Andres, Liliana Apolinario, Nestor Armesto, Andre Cordeiro, Fabio Dominguez, Pablo Guerrero-Rodriguez, Jose Guilherme Milhano; 7 authors. arXiv:2607.00629

Summary: The authors formulate the full in-medium gluon-emission spectrum from a quark-antiquark antenna as differential equations that can be solved numerically. The approach resums medium interactions to all orders while allowing realistic scattering models beyond the harmonic-oscillator approximation.

Why it matters: Coherent radiation and color decoherence shape jet substructure modification in heavy-ion collisions. Removing a restrictive approximation is a meaningful step toward precision jet-quenching theory.

Critical assessment: Strong theory contribution. Its practical value will depend on whether the formalism can be embedded into phenomenological jet calculations without losing the realism it adds.

Phenomenology

Subensemble Acceptance Method 3.0: General Corrections to Cumulants from Exact Conservation Constraints

strong

Roman Poberezhnyuk, Volodymyr A. Kuznietsov, Gregoire Pihan, Volodymyr Vovchenko; 4 authors. arXiv:2607.01783

Summary: SAM-3.0 corrects subsystem cumulants for exact global conservation of multiple charges, including energy. It gives a closed recursion, contains SAM-1.0 and SAM-2.0 as limits, reproduces the exact binomial-acceptance limit, and updates Hydro-EV net-proton baselines for RHIC-BES energies.

Why it matters: Conservation effects are a major baseline issue for fluctuation measurements in QCD critical-point searches. This paper improves the baseline machinery needed before interpreting cumulant deviations as critical behavior.

Critical assessment: Very strong. The formalism is broad and the validation against direct canonical Monte Carlo sampling is exactly the right credibility check; the remaining challenge is careful experimental acceptance and efficiency implementation.

Spin Femtoscopy: A Framework for Revealing Genuine Spin Correlations

promising

Kehao Zhang, Xuan Wang, Xiaofeng Luo; 3 authors. arXiv:2607.00413

Summary: The paper proposes spin-resolved femtoscopic correlation functions to access genuine two-particle spin correlations. Lambda-Lambda pairs and self-analyzing weak decays provide the proof-of-principle route for separating spin populations from femtoscopic mixing.

Why it matters: Heavy-ion spin studies have been dominated by single-particle polarization and vector-meson spin alignment. A viable two-particle spin-correlation observable would add a genuinely new handle on hadronic-stage spin structure.

Critical assessment: Promising and scientifically interesting. I would not call it fully established yet because real-data feasibility will depend on pair statistics, final-state-interaction modeling, and control of spin-dependent acceptance effects.

AI/ML use

Heavy-Flavor Electron Classification Using Hadronic Environment as Point Cloud

strong

Jingyu Zhang, Wanbing He, Long Ma; 3 authors. arXiv:2607.02225

Summary: The authors represent the hadronic environment around semi-leptonic heavy-flavor electrons as a point cloud and compare set-based neural architectures, including transformers. Around 40% efficiency, the classifier reaches roughly 80% purity and improves over a hand-crafted-observable BDT baseline.

Why it matters: Charm-bottom separation is directly useful for heavy-flavor measurements, including heavy-ion analyses where D- and B-origin electrons probe different transport and energy-loss physics.

Critical assessment: Strong ML application because it includes a conventional baseline, an experimentally meaningful working point, and response studies tied to physics observables. The main missing stress tests are generator dependence, detector effects, and high-occupancy heavy-ion environments.

AI/ML use cases in physics

WavePID: Low-energy flavor identification using single-PMT time series in IceCube

strong transfer

IceCube Collaboration: R. Abbasi, M. Ackermann, J. Adams, J. A. Aguilar, M. Ahlers et al.; 419 authors. arXiv:2607.02078

Method and result: WavePID is a template-based log-likelihood-ratio classifier using nanosecond-scale timing observables from individual detector modules. Applied after a graph-neural-network cascade selection, it improves low-energy neutrino flavor identification.

Likely use case: The transferable pattern is hybrid reconstruction: learned morphology plus physics-motivated low-level timing observables. That is relevant for precision-timing detectors, calorimetry, Cherenkov systems, and heavy-ion detector upgrades with timing information.

Critical assessment: Strong because it demonstrates complementarity between ML selection and interpretable detector observables rather than treating the neural network as a black box endpoint.

Local Conformal Predictions for Calibrated Surrogates

strong transfer

Suprio Dubey, Henning Bahl, Anja Butter, Jurgen Hesser, Tilman Plehn; 5 authors. arXiv:2607.01354

Method and result: The paper introduces FALCON, a conformal-prediction method for locally calibrated confidence intervals on neural-network surrogates. The motivating application is LHC scattering-amplitude surrogates for ultra-fast event generation under non-Gaussian systematics.

Likely use case: This is directly useful for HEP surrogate modeling and plausibly transferable to heavy-ion emulator calibration, fast event generation, detector-response surrogates, and uncertainty-aware likelihood workflows.

Critical assessment: Strong because uncertainty calibration is the hard part of production surrogate use. The next evidence needed is local coverage under realistic phase-space sparsity, model mismatch, and downstream inference.

Weak or overclaimed papers worth caution

Frequency Shift Physics-Informed Extreme Learning Machine for Solving High-Frequency Partial Differential Equations

caution

Xiong Xiong, Ruonan Zhai, Zheng Zeng, Sheng Zhou, Rongchun Hu, Zichen Deng; 6 authors. arXiv:2607.01694

Why caution: The paper addresses a real numerical issue, spectral bias in high-frequency PDE solutions, and reports large gains across benchmark equations. But the evidence remains benchmark-centered and the HEP/NP transfer path is generic rather than demonstrated.

Critical assessment: Potentially useful as a numerical-method component, but not yet a strong physics AI/ML result for heavy-ion or HEP workflows. It needs noisy inverse problems, conservation constraints, complex detector geometries, or hydrodynamic benchmarks before the claims become actionable.
