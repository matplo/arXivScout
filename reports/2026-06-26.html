Heavy-Ion Collision Papers

Theory

Applicability of kinetic theory in strongly coupled thermal quantum systems

Mixed

Shile Chen, Shuzhe Shi, Pengfei Zhuang. arXiv:2606.26696

The paper studies one-dimensional interacting lattice spinor theories, including Schwinger and Nambu-Jona-Lasinio models, and computes single- and two-particle momentum distributions at finite temperature. The central claim is that at sufficiently high temperature, two-particle correlations become subdominant relative to single-particle distributions, giving a controlled regime where kinetic theory becomes applicable.

Why it matters: The kinetic-theory assumption sits underneath much of heavy-ion transport modeling, so even simplified strongly coupled tests are useful for thinking about when quasiparticle descriptions can be trusted.

Critical assessment: Scientifically interesting but indirect for phenomenology. The model systems are deliberately simplified, so the work is best read as conceptual support and diagnostics rather than evidence that a particular heavy-ion transport setup is validated.

Experiment

Modification of jet-energy flow in heavy-ion collisions

Strong

ALICE Collaboration. arXiv:2606.20061

ALICE presents first measurements of the jet-energy-flow observable in pp and Pb-Pb collisions, using inclusive charged jets and comparing Pb-Pb modifications to pp references. The result shows significant suppression of energy flow at larger radii, indicating a narrower radial energy pattern in heavy-ion collisions; several generators describe pp, but heavy-ion model comparisons separate JEWEL without recoil from recoil/medium-response variants and JETSCAPE.

Why it matters: This is a genuinely useful experimental constraint on how energy is redistributed inside and around quenched jets, with direct consequences for medium-response and shower-modification modeling.

Critical assessment: Strong and concrete: a new IRC-safe observable, real data, quantified significance, and model discrimination. The remaining caution is that the observable is measured in a specific jet momentum window and charged-jet setup, so theory comparisons need careful matching.

Phenomenology

In-medium QCD splittings beyond the soft, large-Nc and harmonic-oscillator approximations all at once

Strong

Marco Leitao, Jose Guilherme Milhano, Alba Soto-Ontoso. arXiv:2606.26039

This paper reports a complete numerical solution of the BDMPS-Z equations for in-medium QCD splittings, relaxing several standard approximations simultaneously: soft emission, large-Nc color, harmonic-oscillator medium dynamics, and finite-energy simplifications. It finds substantial phase-space regions where the usual approximations deviate, and frames the result as a path toward more precise jet-observable calculations.

Why it matters: Heavy-ion jet phenomenology often inherits uncertainties from analytic approximations; a precise numerical baseline can expose which approximations are harmless and which bias medium-parameter extraction.

Critical assessment: High-value methodology paper. Its impact will depend on integration into full jet calculations and comparison to data, but the problem is important and the approximation audit is exactly the right target.

Mapping jet substructure in heavy-ion collisions with track functions

Strong

Joao Barata, Yeonju Go, Daniel Pablos. arXiv:2606.23971

The authors study track functions, which encode energy flow from an initiating parton into charged hadrons, in heavy-ion jet environments using JEWEL and HYBRID quenching models. Higher moments and cumulants deviate from vacuum baselines, and the size of the modification differs between models, suggesting discrimination power for microscopic jet-medium pictures.

Why it matters: Track functions offer a controlled way to connect charged-particle jet measurements, fragmentation dynamics, and RG evolution in a medium.

Critical assessment: Strong as phenomenological proposal and model-comparison study. The experimental extraction challenges are real, and the model dependence cuts both ways: it is useful for discrimination but also means the observable needs careful unfolding and systematics control.

Studying the QCD Matter produced in Heavy-Ion Collisions using the MUSES Calculation Engine

Strong

Johannes Jahan, Kevin P. Pala, Yumu Yang, Isabella Danhoni, Prachi Garella, et al.; 44 authors, MUSES Collaboration. arXiv:2606.26326

This long collaboration paper documents the Calliope version of the MUSES Calculation Engine for equations of state and heavy-ion modeling workflows. It covers finite-temperature and finite-baryon-density EoS modules, strangeness and charge chemical potentials, thermodynamic derivatives, transport-coefficient interfaces, EoS merging, inversion for hydrodynamics, and example simulations at 7.7, 19.6, and 39 GeV.

Why it matters: BES and future finite-density modeling need reproducible EoS and transport workflows; this paper is a practical bridge between theory inputs and hydrodynamic simulations.

Critical assessment: Strong infrastructure contribution, especially if the promised public code release is timely and usable. The physics conclusions from the example runs should be treated as demonstrations, not final constraints.

Rapidity-even directed flow splitting of protons and antiprotons as a probe of baryon stopping in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

Mixed

Tribhuban Parida, Sandeep Chatterjee. arXiv:2606.27156

The paper uses 3+1D viscous hydrodynamics coupled to hadronic transport to study rapidity-even directed-flow splitting between protons and antiprotons at BES energies. It proposes the midrapidity curvature of the splitting as a discriminator of baryon deposition profiles and baryon diffusion strength.

Why it matters: Baryon stopping is a central uncertainty for finite-density heavy-ion initial conditions, and the proposed curvature observable is experimentally interpretable.

Critical assessment: A focused and useful proposal, but not yet a decisive constraint. The strength will depend on measurement precision and on whether initial-state and hadronic-afterburner systematics can be separated cleanly.

Understanding the Intermittency Signal in RHIC-STAR Data through Modeling

Mixed

Jin Wu, Zhiming Li, Mingmei Xu, Shuyun Yang, Ranran Guo, Yuanfang Wu. arXiv:2606.24436

This study uses an improved hybrid UrQMD+CMC setup to embed critical-like fluctuations into realistic non-critical backgrounds and compare second-order factorial moments point-by-point with STAR BES-I intermittency measurements. The result argues that the current data are compatible only with small, nearly energy-independent critical-like fractions, rather than a strong localized critical-point enhancement.

Why it matters: It addresses a high-stakes observable in the QCD critical-point search and tries to translate measured intermittency into model-level signal strength.

Critical assessment: Useful and appropriately sobering. The conclusion is important if robust, but it remains model-dependent because the embedding of critical-like fluctuations and background treatment control the inference.

AI/ML Use

Ultra-Peripheral Collisions as a Nuclear-Structure Interferometer with Interpretable Multitask Deep Learning

Mixed

Jing-Zong Zhang, Wang-Mei Zha, Lingxiao Wang, Guo-Liang Ma. arXiv:2606.23353

The paper proposes an interpretable multitask deep-learning framework that maps transverse-momentum distributions in coherent J/psi photoproduction to several nuclear-structure indicators. It uses Zr-Zr UPCs to show separation between diffraction-dominated and interference-dominated information and produces analysis-oriented observables.

Why it matters: It is a concrete ML use case in nuclear physics, aimed at an inverse problem where multiple correlated structural quantities matter simultaneously.

Critical assessment: Promising but still simulation-forward. The interpretability angle is relevant, but the decisive test will be robustness to detector effects, backgrounds, generator priors, and realistic high-luminosity uncertainties.

AI/ML Use Cases in Physics

Interpreting "Interpretability" and Explaining "Explainability" in Machine Learning in Physics

Strong

Rikab Gambhir, Luisa Lucie-Smith, Jesse Thaler. arXiv:2606.26228

Method and result: This is a conceptual review rather than a new algorithm. It separates interpretability, meaning structural transparency, from explainability, meaning connection to domain knowledge, and argues that both should be deliberate modeling choices tied to task specification and planned interventions.

Likely HEP/heavy-ion use case: It is directly useful for ML-based event classification, unfolding, anomaly detection, simulation tuning, and surrogate modeling, where "interpretable" claims often blur transparency, physics content, and operational usefulness.

Critical assessment: Strong and transferable. The paper will not solve validation by itself, but it gives HEP/NP analysts a sharper vocabulary for deciding what kind of model understanding is actually needed for a measurement or discovery claim.

Ultra-Peripheral Collisions as a Nuclear-Structure Interferometer with Interpretable Multitask Deep Learning

Mixed

Jing-Zong Zhang, Wang-Mei Zha, Lingxiao Wang, Guo-Liang Ma. arXiv:2606.23353

Method and result: Multitask learning is used to infer multiple nuclear-structure indicators from UPC transverse-momentum spectra, with interpretation tools identifying the kinematic regions most responsible for each inference.

Likely HEP/heavy-ion use case: The template transfers to correlated inverse problems in heavy-ion analysis: simultaneous inference of initial-state geometry, medium response, and nuclear structure from differential distributions.

Critical assessment: Worth watching, but not yet a general solution. The paper appears strongest as a proof of analysis strategy; transferability hinges on robustness to realistic experimental and model uncertainties.

Weak or Overclaimed Papers Worth Caution

Mass-Dependent Non-Extensivity in Tsallis Blast-Wave Fits to Identified Hadron pT Spectra at RHIC and LHC

Caution

C.Y. Tsang, Z. Xu. arXiv:2606.24777

The paper fits identified-hadron spectra across RHIC and LHC energies with extended Tsallis Blast-Wave parameterizations, finding improved chi-squared performance when the non-extensivity parameter varies with particle mass and particle class.

Why caution: The empirical fit improvement may be useful for describing spectra, but the physical interpretation of mass-dependent non-extensivity as kinetic freeze-out behavior is not automatically established by better fit quality. This is a case where the phenomenological flexibility may outrun the strength of the physics conclusion.
