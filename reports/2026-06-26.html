Executive Takeaways

  • The June 26 listing is strongest on finite-density heavy-ion infrastructure and diagnostics: equation-of-state workflows, baryon-stopping observables, and STAR intermittency reinterpretation.
  • Hot-QCD spectral information is a second recurring theme, with meson screening masses tied to lattice-QCD benchmarks and Dyson-Schwinger modeling.

Heavy-Ion Collision Papers

Theory

Applicability of kinetic theory in strongly coupled thermal quantum systems

Mixed

Shile Chen, Shuzhe Shi, Pengfei Zhuang. arXiv:2606.26696

The authors construct one-dimensional interacting lattice spinor theories in momentum space and study finite-temperature single- and two-particle distributions in strongly interacting Schwinger and Nambu-Jona-Lasinio models. Their main claim is that once thermal kinetic energy becomes comparable to the interaction scale, two-particle correlations become subdominant, indicating a regime where kinetic theory is applicable.

Why it matters: Heavy-ion transport rests on kinetic assumptions, so controlled tests of when correlations become subleading are conceptually useful.

Critical assessment: Interesting but indirect. The simplified systems make the paper better as a diagnostic study than as validation of a particular QGP transport implementation.

Axial-Vector Lattice Benchmarks Reveal a Common Medium Response of Meson Screening in Hot QCD

Strong

Jie Ren, Chen Chen, Fei Gao, Si-xue Qin. arXiv:2606.26245

This paper combines lattice-QCD screening-mass benchmarks with a symmetry-preserving Dyson-Schwinger baseline to study axial-vector, vector, and chiral-partner meson channels in hot QCD. A single axial-vector input fixes the medium response, while remaining axial-vector and vector data act as validation tests.

Why it matters: Meson screening masses are clean finite-temperature QCD diagnostics, and this framework connects lattice benchmarks to predicted flavor-dependent spectra.

Critical assessment: Strong within its theory scope because it uses external lattice information and validation rather than pure model tuning. Direct heavy-ion phenomenology remains downstream.

Experiment

Modification of jet-energy flow in heavy-ion collisions

Strong

ALICE Collaboration. arXiv:2606.20061

ALICE reports a first measurement of the jet-energy-flow observable in pp and Pb-Pb collisions, finding significant suppression of energy flow at larger radii in heavy-ion data. Comparisons to JEWEL variants and JETSCAPE give useful discrimination power for recoil and medium-response pictures.

Why it matters: The measurement constrains how quenched jets redistribute energy radially, a central question for jet-medium dynamics.

Critical assessment: Strong and data-driven. Theory comparisons still need careful matching to charged jets and the measured momentum range.

Phenomenology

Studying the QCD Matter produced in Heavy-Ion Collisions using the MUSES Calculation Engine

Strong

Johannes Jahan, Kevin P. Pala, Yumu Yang, Isabella Danhoni, Prachi Garella, et al.; 45 listed authors, MUSES Collaboration. arXiv:2606.26326

The Calliope release of the MUSES Calculation Engine is presented as a finite-density equation-of-state and workflow platform for heavy-ion modeling. It includes EoS modules spanning lattice-QCD and phenomenological approaches, chemical-potential extensions, thermodynamic derivatives, transport-coefficient interfaces, EoS merging, inversion, and example viscous-hydrodynamic simulations at 7.7, 19.6, and 39 GeV.

Why it matters: Reproducible finite-density EoS workflows are essential for Beam Energy Scan interpretation and for testing critical-point scenarios in hydrodynamics.

Critical assessment: Strong infrastructure paper. The example simulations are demonstrations; the real payoff depends on public code availability, documentation, and adoption in model-to-data analyses.

Rapidity-even directed flow splitting of protons and antiprotons as a probe of baryon stopping in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

Mixed

Tribhuban Parida, Sandeep Chatterjee. arXiv:2606.27156

Using 3+1D viscous hydrodynamics coupled to hadronic transport, this paper studies rapidity-even directed-flow splitting between protons and antiprotons at BES energies. The proposed midrapidity curvature of the splitting is presented as a discriminator of baryon deposition profiles and diffusion strength.

Why it matters: Baryon stopping is a central initial-condition uncertainty in finite-density heavy-ion collisions, and the observable is experimentally concrete.

Critical assessment: Useful and focused, but not yet decisive. It will need precision data and careful accounting of afterburner and initial-state degeneracies.

Understanding the Intermittency Signal in RHIC-STAR Data through Modeling

Mixed

Jin Wu, Zhiming Li, Mingmei Xu, Shuyun Yang, Ranran Guo, Yuanfang Wu. arXiv:2606.24436

The authors embed critical-like fluctuations into realistic non-critical backgrounds using an improved hybrid UrQMD+CMC model and compare second-order factorial moments with STAR BES-I measurements. They infer that current data favor only a small and nearly energy-independent critical-like contribution.

Why it matters: This is a useful counterweight to overly eager readings of intermittency as a localized critical-point signal.

Critical assessment: Valuable but model-dependent. The conclusion depends strongly on how the critical component is embedded and how the background is modeled.

Mapping jet substructure in heavy-ion collisions with track functions

Strong

Joao Barata, Yeonju Go, Daniel Pablos. arXiv:2606.23971

This recent phenomenology study uses JEWEL and HYBRID to examine how track-function moments and cumulants change in quenched jets. The modifications differ significantly across models, suggesting discrimination power for jet-medium interaction mechanisms.

Why it matters: Track functions connect charged-hadron measurements to controlled QFT evolution and may become a sharp jet-substructure diagnostic.

Critical assessment: Strong proposal, with experimental extraction as the key practical challenge.

AI/ML Use

Ultra-Peripheral Collisions as a Nuclear-Structure Interferometer with Interpretable Multitask Deep Learning

Mixed

Jing-Zong Zhang, Wang-Mei Zha, Lingxiao Wang, Guo-Liang Ma. arXiv:2606.23353

The paper uses interpretable multitask deep learning to map coherent J/psi photoproduction transverse-momentum spectra in ultra-peripheral collisions onto nuclear-structure indicators. It separates diffraction- and interference-dominated information and frames UPCs as a nuclear-structure inverse problem.

Why it matters: This is a concrete ML application inside nuclear/HEP analysis rather than a generic classifier pasted onto physics data.

Critical assessment: Promising but simulation-forward. Robustness to detector effects, backgrounds, generator priors, and luminosity assumptions will determine whether the approach becomes analysis-grade.

AI/ML Use Cases in Physics

Interpreting "Interpretability" and Explaining "Explainability" in Machine Learning in Physics

Strong

Rikab Gambhir, Luisa Lucie-Smith, Jesse Thaler. arXiv:2606.26228

Method and result: This review distinguishes interpretability, meaning structural transparency, from explainability, meaning a connection to scientific domain knowledge. It emphasizes task specification and planned interventions as part of model design.

Likely HEP/heavy-ion use case: The framing transfers directly to event classification, unfolding, anomaly detection, surrogate modeling, detector calibration, and simulation tuning.

Critical assessment: Strong and broadly transferable. It is conceptual rather than algorithmic, but the conceptual cleanup is valuable because HEP/NP papers often use interpretability language too loosely.

Machine Learning-based Unfolding for Cross Section Measurements in the Presence of Nuisance Parameters

Strong

Huanbiao Zhu, Krish Desai, Mikael Kuusela, Vinicius Mikuni, Benjamin Nachman, Larry Wasserman. arXiv:2512.07074 (replacement)

Method and result: The paper extends OmniFold into Profile OmniFold, incorporating nuisance parameters into ML-based unfolding. It is demonstrated on a Gaussian example and a simulated CMS case study.

Likely HEP/heavy-ion use case: This is directly relevant to heavy-ion and HEP differential measurements where detector corrections, model uncertainties, and correlated nuisance parameters must be profiled rather than ignored.

Critical assessment: Strong methodological relevance. Heavy-ion deployment will need stress tests under high multiplicity, background subtraction, and centrality-dependent systematics.

Weak or Overclaimed Papers Worth Caution

Thermodynamical analysis of QGP using effective PNJL model with Quasiparticle approach

Caution

Yogesh Kumar, Himanshu Aggarwal, R. S. Laishram, Poonam Jain, Om Prakash, et al.; 10 authors. arXiv:2606.26956

The paper extends a two-flavor PNJL setup with quasiparticle masses and reports improved agreement with lattice-QCD thermodynamic observables at zero chemical potential and magnetic field.

Why caution: The topic is relevant, but the claims appear mostly baseline-fitting and model-improvement oriented. Without stronger uncertainty treatment, independent validation, or new phenomenological leverage, it should not be overread as a robust QGP equation-of-state advance.

Mass-Dependent Non-Extensivity in Tsallis Blast-Wave Fits to Identified Hadron pT Spectra at RHIC and LHC

Caution

C.Y. Tsang, Z. Xu. arXiv:2606.24777

The paper fits identified-hadron spectra with extended Tsallis Blast-Wave parameterizations and finds better fit quality when non-extensivity varies by particle mass or class.

Why caution: Improved empirical description may be useful, but flexible parameterization can mimic physics. The interpretation of mass-dependent non-extensivity as freeze-out dynamics needs independent support beyond chi-squared improvement.
