Daily arXiv Physics Scout

2026-07-04 Briefing

Heavy quarkonium as a small-system hot-medium probe, heavy-ion fusion constraints on deformation, TMD phenomenology, and physics-ML methods with credible detector or simulation transfer paths.

Executive takeaways

  • The strongest heavy-ion-relevant paper is the quarkonium medium study. Its value is the focus on excited-to-ground-state ratios, which suppress shared cold-nuclear-matter uncertainties and can compare small and large systems in one framework.
  • Low-energy heavy-ion fusion also produced a solid experimental-theory item. The 140Ce deformation study combines new fusion data, literature data, Bayesian extraction, and coupled-channels validation rather than relying on one fit.
  • HEP-facing ML is unusually concrete today. The heavy-flavor electron point-cloud classifier reports an experimentally meaningful working point and interprets learned responses through physics-motivated observables.
  • Detector timing remains a high-value transfer idea. WavePID is not a neural model, but it is a strong example of physics-informed classification layered onto a GNN-selected sample; this style can transfer to timing-rich HEP and nuclear detectors.
  • Generic physics-ML claims need domain stress tests. PINN optimization and MLIP optimizer results are promising, but neither should be treated as proven for heavy-ion hydro, transport, detector simulation, or uncertainty-controlled inference without targeted validation.

Heavy-ion collision papers

Theory

Probing hot QCD medium with heavy quarkonium in small and large collision systems

strong

Jiamin Liu, Baoyi Chen; 2 authors. arXiv:2607.02042

Summary: The authors use a time-dependent Schroedinger-equation framework with complex in-medium heavy-quark potentials to evolve charmonium and bottomonium states. They describe excited-to-ground-state suppression ratios in p-Pb at 8.16 TeV and extend the same framework to bottomonium nuclear-modification-factor ratios in Pb-Pb at 5.02 TeV.

Why it matters: Quarkonium ratios are attractive because many common cold-nuclear-matter and production uncertainties cancel. If the same dynamics can account for charmonium and bottomonium across small and large systems, it strengthens the case that final-state hot-medium effects are measurable even in high-multiplicity p-Pb.

Critical assessment: Strong and on-scope, with a clean observable strategy and compact execution. The main caution is interpretive: agreement with multiplicity-dependent ratios supports a hot-medium explanation, but it does not by itself exclude all competing small-system mechanisms or initial-state effects.

Experiment

Determining the dynamic deformation of 140Ce by constraining coupled-channels parameters for fusion

strong

Chandra Kumar, Rohan Biswas, J. Gehlot, Gonika, A. Parihari, N. Madhavan, A. Vinayak, Amritraj Mahato, S. Nath; 9 authors. arXiv:2607.01309

Summary: This paper combines new 16O+140Ce fusion cross sections, literature 36S+140Ce data, Gaussian analytic-barrier modeling, chi-square minimization, Bayesian analysis, and coupled-channels calculations. It extracts beta2 and beta3 deformation parameters and tests them against 28Si+140Ce fusion and barrier-distribution data including two-neutron transfer.

Why it matters: Although this is low-energy heavy-ion fusion rather than ultrarelativistic QGP physics, it is a careful heavy-ion reaction study with useful methodology for constraining nuclear structure through reaction observables.

Critical assessment: Scientifically solid: the cross-system consistency check and Bayesian Model Averaging make the deformation extraction more convincing than a single-channel fit. Its relevance to QGP-oriented heavy-ion work is indirect, so I would treat it as a strong nuclear-reaction item rather than a central hot-QCD result.

Phenomenology

Event-axis TMD measurements in e+e- and SIDIS

strong adjacent

Daniel Diaz Fernandez, Patricia Andrea Gutierrez Garcia, Ignazio Scimemi, Wouter Waalewijn; 4 authors. arXiv:2607.02438

Summary: The paper develops the operator and phenomenological treatment of TMD fragmentation measurements relative to the thrust axis in e+e- and analogous 1-jettiness observables in SIDIS. It includes soft-factor checks, resummation, a nonperturbative model, implementation in artemide, and first validation against Pythia8.3 simulated e+e- data.

Why it matters: This is not a heavy-ion paper, but it is relevant to the QCD toolkit used by nuclear and collider phenomenology, especially for fragmentation and transverse-momentum structure. It is also cross-listed to nucl-ex and nucl-th.

Critical assessment: Technically strong and likely useful for precision QCD. The heavy-ion connection is indirect, so I would not overstate its immediate relevance to QGP phenomenology.

AI/ML use

Heavy-Flavor Electron Classification Using Hadronic Environment as Point Cloud

strong

Jingyu Zhang, Wanbing He, Long Ma; 3 authors. arXiv:2607.02225

Summary: The authors represent the hadronic environment around semi-leptonic heavy-flavor electrons as a point cloud and compare set-based ML architectures, including transformer models, for separating charm- and bottom-origin electrons. At about 40% efficiency, the classifier reaches roughly 80% purity and improves over a hand-crafted-observable BDT baseline.

Why it matters: Charm-bottom separation is directly useful for heavy-flavor measurements in high-energy and heavy-ion collisions, where D- and B-origin electrons probe different mass-dependent energy-loss and transport physics.

Critical assessment: Strong for an ML application because it includes a conventional baseline, an experimentally relevant working point, and feature-perturbation/observable studies that probe what the model learned. The next test is domain robustness: generator dependence, detector effects, event occupancy, and performance in realistic heavy-ion backgrounds.

AI/ML use cases in physics

WavePID: Low-energy flavor identification using single-PMT time series in IceCube

strong transfer

IceCube Collaboration: R. Abbasi, M. Ackermann, J. Adams, J. A. Aguilar, M. Ahlers et al.; large collaboration. arXiv:2607.02078

Method and result: WavePID is a template-based log-likelihood-ratio classifier using nanosecond-scale timing observables from individual optical modules. Evaluated on a cascade-enriched sample selected by a graph neural network, it improves flavor-identification performance over the GNN-only selection.

Likely use case: The transfer value is methodological: physics-informed low-level timing features can complement learned morphology classifiers. This is relevant to timing layers, Cherenkov detectors, calorimeters with precision timing, and future reconstruction pipelines in HEP and nuclear experiments.

Critical assessment: Strong because it demonstrates a hybrid analysis pattern rather than replacing physics insight with a black box. It is not a generic ML architecture paper, but its validation logic is exactly the kind HEP/NP ML workflows need.

An Optimisation Framework for the Well-Conditioned Training of Physics-Informed Neural Networks

promising but needs stress tests

Joseph Webb, Sadok Jerad, Coralia Cartis; 3 authors. arXiv:2607.02194

Method and result: The paper introduces DSGNAR, a doubly-sketched Gauss-Newton method with adaptive regularization and step-length control for PINNs. The abstract reports very large improvements in accuracy and speed across nonlinear, chaotic, multiscale, high-dimensional, and Navier-Stokes benchmarks, with code available.

Likely use case: If the optimizer is robust, it could help train PDE surrogates or inverse solvers for hydrodynamics, transport, detector fields, or calibration problems where standard PINN training is unstable.

Critical assessment: Worth tracking because it attacks a real PINN bottleneck. The claims are large, so transfer to HEP/heavy-ion workflows should be withheld until it is tested on noisy data, stiff multi-physics systems, conservation constraints, and uncertainty-aware inference.

Beyond Adam: SOAP and Muon for Faster, Label-Efficient Training of Machine Learning Interatomic Potentials

useful adjacent

Gil Harari, Yoel Zimmermann, Ola Tangen Kulseng, Laura Zichi, Chuin Wei Tan, Marc L. Descoteaux, Boris Kozinsky; 7 authors. arXiv:2607.02499

Method and result: The authors compare matrix-structured optimizers, including SOAP, Muon, and SOAP-Muon, against Adam for NequIP and Allegro interatomic-potential training. SOAP and SOAP-Muon reportedly improve convergence and final accuracy, especially with partial force supervision.

Likely use case: The most credible transfer path is not directly to heavy-ion phenomenology, but to scientific ML training practice: equivariant models, sparse labels, force-like supervision, and expensive simulation labels all have analogues in nuclear many-body and detector-surrogate workflows.

Critical assessment: The optimizer question is practical and often under-tested, which makes this useful. Transfer is indirect, however; gains for molecular MLIPs do not automatically imply gains for collider-event models, hydrodynamic emulators, or nuclear-structure networks.

Weak or overclaimed papers worth caution

Frequency Shift Physics-Informed Extreme Learning Machine for Solving High-Frequency Partial Differential Equations

caution

Xiong Xiong, Ruonan Zhai, Zheng Zeng, Sheng Zhou, Rongchun Hu, Zichen Deng; 6 authors. arXiv:2607.01694

Why caution: The paper targets a real issue, spectral bias in high-frequency PDE solutions, and reports one-to-five-order benchmark improvements over existing PIELM variants. But the transfer path to HEP or nuclear physics is generic, and the abstract-level evidence does not establish robustness for noisy inverse problems, conservation-law constraints, detector geometries, or heavy-ion hydrodynamics.

Critical assessment: Potentially useful as a numerical-method component, but not yet strong enough to highlight as a physics AI/ML use case for this archive. I would treat it as a method to revisit only after code/data release and domain-specific benchmarks.
