AI/ML Use Cases in Physics

Machine Can Automatically Discover Parametric Functions to Model HEP Data

strong

Ho Fung Tsoi, Dylan Rankin, Cecile Caillol, Miles Cranmer, Sridhara Dasu, Javier Duarte, Philip Harris, Elliot Lipeles. arXiv:2607.19750

Method and result: SymbolFit combines symbolic regression with uncertainty modeling to automate analytic function discovery for binned HEP spectra. On CMS and ATLAS Run 2 dijet spectra, the authors report 560 seeded runs across seven configurations, more than 1000 functions with chi2/NDF near 1, and 111 rediscoveries of the published dijet and UA2 functional forms.

Likely use case: Directly useful for HEP resonance searches and background modeling. For heavy-ion work, the most plausible use is not replacing physics models, but stress-testing empirical fit choices in spectra, correlation functions, UPC distributions, and detector-background parameterizations.

Critical assessment: Strong. The task is real, the benchmark is HEP-native, and rediscovering established functions is a meaningful sanity check. The main risk is analyst over-trust: symbolic regression can produce plausible formulas that fit a window but extrapolate poorly, so control regions and systematic envelopes remain essential.

Hard Guarantees at a Measured Price: Entropy-Stable Learned Finite Volumes for Compressible Flow

strong

Denis Gueyffier. arXiv:2607.20171

Method and result: Builds an admissibility-preserving, entropy-stable learned finite-volume scheme for two-dimensional Euler flow on unstructured meshes. The paper is unusually careful about evaluation: fixed protocols, falsification clauses, negative controls, factor decomposition, and iso-cost comparisons. The guaranteed variant completes 36 of 36 rollouts with zero negativity events, while learned gains appear mainly in selected wall-boundary cases.

Likely use case: Transfer value is high for heavy-ion hydrodynamic surrogate design, especially because positivity, stability, and cost-normalized comparisons matter more than raw neural-network accuracy. The immediate equations are not relativistic viscous hydrodynamics, but the evaluation philosophy is very relevant.

Critical assessment: Strong as methodology. The paper is valuable partly because it reports where learning does not pay: the unlearned guaranteed skeleton is often strongest at equal mesh, and learned components help selectively. That honesty makes the result more credible than most neural-solver papers.

Label-Free Finite-Volume-Residual Training of Attention Graph Neural Networks for Coupled Thermo-Fluid Fields

mixed

Tianyu Li, Zhiwei Cao, Qingang Zhang, Ruihang Wang, Binyang Song, Yonggang Wen. arXiv:2607.20321

Method and result: Trains an attention graph neural network by minimizing finite-volume residuals of governing equations directly on the mesh, avoiding labeled CFD solution data. The paper reports 2.3-2.8% all-field nRMSE on two steady benchmarks and better accuracy than a supervised baseline in two transient parametric cases without paying the conventional data-generation cost.

Likely use case: Potentially useful for simulation campaigns where labeled high-fidelity fields are expensive: detector thermal simulations, accelerator component modeling, and eventually heavy-ion hydrodynamic emulators if adapted to relativistic equations and realistic freeze-out observables.

Critical assessment: Promising but mixed. The label-free residual idea is attractive, and mesh-native training is a good fit for scientific codes. The abstract does not yet establish robustness under shocks, stiff source terms, conservation-sensitive observables, or out-of-distribution geometries that would matter for HEP production use.

Interval and fuzzy physics-augmented neural networks (iPANN and fPANN) for uncertainty quantification and propagation in constitutive modeling

mixed

Somesh Pratap Singh, Govinda Anantha Padmanabha, Jingye Tan, Steven Yang, Reese E. Jones, D. Thomas Seidl, Nikolaos Bouklas. arXiv:2607.20339

Method and result: Introduces interval and fuzzy physics-augmented neural networks for hyperelastic constitutive modeling with sparse, noisy, or heterogeneous data. The models learn lower, mean, and upper energy-density branches, impose mechanistic constraints such as objectivity and consistency, encourage interpretable sparse energies, and propagate bounds through finite-element simulations.

Likely use case: The nearest HEP/NP transfer is uncertainty-aware surrogate modeling: nuclear equations of state, detector-material response, or transport coefficients where sparse data and uncertainty propagation matter. It is less directly applicable to collider event analysis than the symbolic-regression paper.

Critical assessment: Mixed but worth tracking. The uncertainty framing is serious and more useful than point-prediction-only surrogates. Transfer to heavy-ion physics would require domain-specific constraints and validation against observables, not just reuse of the interval/fuzzy architecture.

Weak or Overclaimed Papers Worth Caution

PG-KINN: A Physics-Informed Petrov-Galerkin Kolmogorov-Arnold Network for Solving Forward and Inverse PDEs

caution

Amirhossein Sadr, Nima Soltani, Vahideh Moghtadaiee, Aida Pakniyat, Dara Rahmati, Saeid Gorgin. arXiv:2607.20378

Why it may look promising: It combines a Petrov-Galerkin weak formulation with KAN trial spaces and claims broad wins over MLP and KAN baselines across mechanics benchmarks, including inverse parameter identification and complex geometries.

Why caution is warranted: The method may be numerically interesting, but the HEP/heavy-ion transfer path is speculative from the abstract alone. The strongest claims are framed around general computational mechanics, not conservation-sensitive collider or nuclear workflows. I would not treat it as a strong physics AI/ML result for this scout until it shows domain-relevant benchmarks, ablations, and uncertainty behavior.
