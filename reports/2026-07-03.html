Heavy-Ion Collision Papers

Theory

Probing hot QCD medium with heavy quarkonium in small and large collision systems

strong

Jiamin Liu, Baoyi Chen. arXiv:2607.02042. Category: Theory / Phenomenology.

Summary. The paper uses a time-dependent Schrodinger-equation treatment with in-medium complex heavy-quark potentials to model charmonium and bottomonium evolution in p-Pb and Pb-Pb collisions. Its central observable is the excited-to-ground-state quarkonium yield ratio, chosen because it should reduce common cold-nuclear-matter systematics. The authors report agreement with multiplicity-dependent suppression of Upsilon and psi ratios in p-Pb at 8.16 TeV and apply the same framework to bottomonium nuclear-modification-factor ratios in Pb-Pb at 5.02 TeV.

Why it matters. If robust, quarkonium ratios offer a compact test of whether small systems contain a transient hot QCD medium, while connecting directly to large-system quarkonium suppression phenomenology.

Critical assessment. Scientifically strong as a focused phenomenological study: the observable choice is well motivated and the cross-system comparison is valuable. The main caveat is that the claim of hot-medium formation in small systems remains model-dependent; a deeper read should check sensitivity to the assumed potential, initial production, and residual cold-matter effects.

Abstract. The yield ratios of different heavy quarkonium states serve as sensitive probes of final-state interactions in relativistic nuclear collisions, as they effectively cancel out common cold-nuclear-matter effects. To quantify hot QCD medium effects in small collision systems, such as proton-nucleus collisions, the authors simulate real-time bottomonium and charmonium evolution with in-medium complex heavy-quark potentials and compare to p-Pb and Pb-Pb data.

Experiment

Determining the dynamic deformation of 140Ce by constraining coupled-channels parameters for fusion

strong

Chandra Kumar, Rohan Biswas, J. Gehlot, Gonika, A. Parihari, N. Madhavan, A. Vinayak, Amritraj Mahato, S. Nath. 9 authors. arXiv:2607.01309. Category: Experiment / Phenomenology.

Summary. This work studies dynamic deformation in 140Ce using 16O and 36S heavy-ion fusion reactions. It combines new 16O+140Ce fusion cross sections, literature 36S+140Ce data, Gaussian analytic-barrier methods, coupled-channels calculations, chi-square minimization, and Bayesian model averaging. The extracted beta2 and beta3 values are then tested against 28Si+140Ce fusion and barrier-distribution data including two-neutron transfer.

Why it matters. Fusion-barrier distributions are a sensitive probe of nuclear structure in heavy-ion reactions, and the multi-system validation gives the deformation extraction more credibility than a single-channel fit.

Critical assessment. Strong within its low-energy heavy-ion niche. The work is not directly QGP physics, but it is a careful collision-based nuclear-structure study with real experimental leverage and quantified parameter extraction.

Abstract. The authors combine experimental data, a Gaussian analytic-barrier framework, and coupled-channels calculations to constrain dynamic deformation of 140Ce in heavy-ion fusion. They report beta2 = 0.09 +/- 0.03 and beta3 = 0.18 +/- 0.02 from Bayesian model averaging, and show that the parameters help reproduce 28Si+140Ce fusion observables when pair-neutron transfer is included.

AI/ML Use

Heavy-Flavor Electron Classification Using Hadronic Environment as Point Cloud

strong

Jingyu Zhang, Wanbing He, Long Ma. 3 authors. arXiv:2607.02225. Category: AI/ML use.

Summary. The paper represents the hadronic environment around heavy-flavor electrons as a point cloud and compares several set-based architectures, including Transformer models, for charm-versus-bottom electron classification. At about 40% efficiency, the method reaches roughly 80% purity and improves over a hand-crafted-observable BDT baseline. Perturbation and observable-response studies indicate that the model uses geometric and topological information rather than just memorizing high-level proxies.

Why it matters. Charm/bottom electron separation is directly relevant to open-heavy-flavor measurements in pp, pA, and AA collisions, where better flavor tagging can sharpen energy-loss and transport interpretations.

Critical assessment. Strong for an ML application paper: it has an explicit physics target, an interpretable input representation, a relevant baseline, and some diagnostic studies. Remaining risk is realism: detector effects, generator dependence, and domain shift will determine whether the gains survive in an experimental analysis.

Abstract. Electrons from semileptonic charm and bottom decays are important probes in high-energy collisions, but separating their origin is difficult. The authors use point-cloud descriptions of the hadronic environment and set-based ML models, finding improved classification over a BDT baseline and evidence that learned representations capture nontrivial geometric/topological information.

AI/ML Use Cases In Physics

WavePID: Low-energy flavor identification using single-PMT time series in IceCube

strong

R. Abbasi, M. Ackermann, J. Adams, J. A. Aguilar, M. Ahlers, et al. 419 authors. arXiv:2607.02078. Category: AI/ML-adjacent detector analysis.

Method and result. WavePID is a template-based log-likelihood-ratio classifier using nanosecond-scale single-PMT timing information: distance to reconstructed vertex, early-charge fraction, and module-to-module time differences. Evaluated on a cascade-enriched sample selected by a graph neural network, it improves cascade purity and classification performance over the GNN alone.

Likely HEP/heavy-ion use case. The transfer value is not the IceCube topology itself, but the design principle: use physics-motivated low-level timing observables as complementary information to neural event classifiers. That idea is relevant for timing layers, calorimeter pulse-shape analyses, pileup mitigation, and future detector design studies.

Critical assessment. Strong and practically grounded, with simulation-based physics interpretation via Geant4. It is not an end-to-end ML novelty paper, but it is a good example of augmenting a neural classifier with interpretable detector observables.

Do Physics Foundation Models Learn Generalizable Physics? A Bias-Aware Benchmark Across Physical Regimes and Distribution Shifts

mixed but useful

Mengdi Chu, Yang Liu, Ayan Biswas, Han-Wei Shen. 4 authors. arXiv:2605.29283. Category: AI/ML use cases in physics.

Method and result. The paper benchmarks five physics-foundation-model architectures and multiple pretrained variants across eight physical dynamics, three training-data mixtures, and 25 test regimes, yielding 60,000 measurements. Its main conclusion is skeptical: current models behave as conditional generalists whose performance depends on regime, temporal scale, initial-condition complexity, data mix, size, and architecture.

Likely HEP/heavy-ion use case. The benchmark logic transfers well to surrogate modeling for hydrodynamics, transport, detector response, and simulation acceleration: average performance is not enough if the surrogate fails under new centralities, beam energies, geometries, nuisance settings, or phase-space corners.

Critical assessment. Useful and timely, especially because it attacks overbroad foundation-model claims. It remains a methods benchmark rather than a HEP/nuclear demonstration, so the practical transfer value depends on adopting similarly regime-aware evaluations in domain-specific surrogate studies.

Weak Or Overclaimed Papers Worth Caution

Hyperstatistics

caution

Lucas Squillante, Samuel M. Soares, Constantino Tsallis, Mariano de Souza. 4 authors. arXiv:2604.24783. Category: Caution.

Why it may look relevant. The paper is cross-listed to hep-ex, hep-th, nucl-th, accelerator physics, instrumentation, and data analysis, and the abstract mentions midrapidity p-Pb data at the LHC as one demonstration.

Why caution is warranted. From the abstract, the work appears to present a very broad generalized-statistics construction illustrated across capacitor discharge, cryostat pressure decay, p-Pb data, and turbulence. That breadth makes the heavy-ion connection look illustrative rather than diagnostic; without a targeted comparison to established heavy-ion baselines and uncertainties, it should not be treated as strong collision phenomenology.
